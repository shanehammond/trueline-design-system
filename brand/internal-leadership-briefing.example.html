01

Section title — lead paragraph pattern

Open every briefing with a single p.tl-lead line stating what the document is and the period it covers. This is the only place a 17px lead paragraph is used; the rest of the body sits at 14.5px.

Follow the lead with one or two supporting paragraphs that frame the rest of the document — what's in scope, who the audience is, and what's expected of them (review, decide, action).

02

Priority cards + framework strip

Use a .priority-grid with two .priority-card children to spotlight the top one or two themes of the period. Cards have a 3px grey left bar; the eyebrow ord above the uppercase name above a single-sentence description.

Priority 01
Priority Name A

One-sentence description of the first major priority. Keep it under 18 words.

Priority 02
Priority Name B

One-sentence description of the second priority. Cards work in pairs; for three or more themes use a list.

Below the cards, a .framework strip can map the priorities to a four-stage operating model. Use four equal cells; uppercase 18px stage words; 10px tracked ord labels above.

03

Status table with chips

Use a .tl-table with chip-based status cells for branch / unit / workstream snapshots. Column heads are uppercase 10px tracked on grey-05. The first column is a nowrap label in uppercase tracked medium weight (.branch class).

Unit Focus area Status Watch point
Unit A Description of this unit's current focus area On Track What to keep an eye on for this unit
Unit B Description of this unit's current focus area Monitor What to keep an eye on for this unit
Unit C Description of this unit's current focus area Action What to keep an eye on for this unit
Unit D Description of this unit's current focus area On Track What to keep an eye on for this unit

Chip variants: .chip.on-track (green), .chip.monitor (amber), .chip.action (red). Use sparingly — the red chip is the only place red appears in an internal-mode document, and only as a status signal.

04

Two-column decisions grid

Use .two-col to pair "what was agreed" against "what still needs a decision". The right column uses .sub-head.is-muted for a softer grey treatment that signals "not yet locked".

What was agreed
  • Decision point one — short, complete sentence.
  • Decision point two — short, complete sentence.
  • Decision point three — short, complete sentence.
  • Decision point four — short, complete sentence.
Still needs a decision
  • Open question one — what needs to be resolved.
  • Open question two — what needs to be resolved.
  • Open question three — what needs to be resolved.
05

Callout + grouped sub-heads

Use .callout for the single most important takeaway in a section — a grey-05 panel with a 3px grey left rule. Use sparingly; one per section maximum, and only when the line earns the emphasis.

Lead with the single most important point of this section. One or two sentences. The bold lead is the headline; the rest provides just enough context to act on it.

Below the callout, group related lists under .sub-head labels. The dash glyph (a 18×2px grey bar) is generated by the CSS — don't add it manually.

Sub-head — locked items
  • Item one — a complete, action-clear sentence.
  • Item two — a complete, action-clear sentence.
  • Item three — bold a key term if it earns the emphasis.
Sub-head — immediate actions
  • Action one — what, by when, who owns it.
  • Action two — what, by when, who owns it.
Sub-head — still to decide
  • Open question one — what needs to land before the next briefing.
  • Open question two — keep the list short; if it's longer than five items, the section is doing too much.
06

Grouped sub-sections

When one section needs to cover two related-but-distinct themes, stack .sub-head blocks. Don't make these their own numbered sections unless they're independently scannable.

Sub-theme A
  • Point one — short, complete sentence.
  • Point two — short, complete sentence with a strong term if it earns it.
  • Point three — short, complete sentence.
Sub-theme B
  • Point one — short, complete sentence.
  • Point two — short, complete sentence.
  • Point three — short, complete sentence.

08

Two-column grid — repeated use

The same .two-col pattern from §04 can recur further down a document. Multiple two-col grids per briefing is fine — the pattern is the workhorse of decisions-vs-open-questions layouts.

Decisions made
  • Decision one — short, complete sentence.
  • Decision two — short, complete sentence.
  • Decision three — short, complete sentence.
  • Decision four — short, complete sentence.
Pending decisions
  • Open question one — what trade-off is being weighed.
  • Open question two — what trade-off is being weighed.
  • Open question three — what trade-off is being weighed.
09

Plain dash list

When a section is a single flat list of points, drop straight to .tl-list. Bold a key name or term if it earns the emphasis, otherwise leave the line plain.

  • Item one — short, complete sentence carrying the point.
  • Item two — date or status if relevant: DD MMM YYYY.
  • Item three — short, complete sentence.
  • Plain unbolded item — works just as well when there's no headline term to lift.
  • Plain unbolded item — keep total list length below ten where possible.
10

Open-items table — priority + owner

Close every briefing with a single open-items table — what was raised but left without an owner, timeline, or decision. Use .tl-table.open-items with three columns: item / priority chip / owner. This is the action register.

Open item Priority Suggested owner
Open question one — short headline of the decision needed High Owner name
Open question two — short headline of the decision needed High Owner name
Open question three — short headline of the decision needed High Owner A + Owner B
Open question four — short headline of the decision needed Medium Owner name
Open question five — short headline of the decision needed Medium Owner name
Open question six — short headline of the decision needed Low Owner name

Priority chip variants: .chip.p-high (red dot), .chip.p-medium (amber), .chip.p-low (grey). Order rows by priority then by date raised — High first.

11

Closing note (optional)

If the briefing leads to a follow-on action (build something, schedule a session, escalate to a stakeholder), close with a short numbered or dash list of next steps. Keep it scannable — no more than five lines.

  • Next step one — what, by when, who owns the next move.
  • Next step two — what, by when, who owns the next move.
  • Next step three — what, by when, who owns the next move.
