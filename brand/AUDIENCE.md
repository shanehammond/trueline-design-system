# Trueline target audience

Who Trueline is talking to. Read this before writing any customer-facing copy — the segment shapes the angle, the proof you choose, and the language you reach for.

Source: *Trueline Target Audience Benchmark Report (May 2026)* and *Marketing Update 2026 Onwards §6*.

## Primary avatar: the house-proud lifestyle upgrader

> **Australian homeowners who want to create a better outdoor lifestyle and are willing to pay for a properly designed, professionally managed, long-lasting result.**

- **Who they are** — Established homeowners, couples, families or empty nesters, typically 35–70. They have invested emotionally and financially in their home and want the outdoor area to feel like a proper extension of it.
- **What they want** — Shade, weather protection, entertaining space, vehicle protection, better use of the backyard, improved street appeal, and a result that looks intentional.
- **What they fear** — Poor workmanship, council problems, delays, hidden costs, an ugly add-on, wrong product choice, and being left to coordinate too much themselves.
- **Their benchmark statement** — *"I want an outdoor space that improves how we live at home — but I need someone reliable to design it properly, manage the tricky bits, and build something that lasts."*
- **Decision shortcut** — They are not asking *"Who is cheapest?"*. They are asking ***"Who can I trust to get this right?"***

**Trigger moments** — Summer heat, rain ruining plans, a new caravan/boat/car, a renovation phase, retirement lifestyle planning, family gatherings, or finally deciding the backyard is underused.

## The five segments

Sharpen targeting by segment when you can identify which one you're writing to. If you can't, default to Segment 1 (largest, strongest current fit).

### 1. Outdoor entertaining family

- **Motivation** — Make the backyard more comfortable, usable, weather-protected and connected to family life.
- **Decision drivers** — Design advice, visual examples, shade, airflow, rain protection, finish quality, and confidence the structure will suit the home.
- **Emotional hook** — *"Create the outdoor room your family will actually use."*
- **Likely project** — Insulated patio, flyover roof, gable roof, verandah, or outdoor living extension.
- **Best proof** — Before-and-after projects, family-use imagery, all-weather comfort, testimonials.
- **Risk** — If messaging gets too technical, this segment won't feel the lifestyle benefit strongly enough.

### 2. Premium home improver

- **Motivation** — Improve lifestyle and property appeal with a result that feels considered, designed and long-lasting.
- **Decision drivers** — Custom design, premium materials, matched colours, architectural confidence, warranties, experienced consultants.
- **Emotional hook** — *"An outdoor space that looks like it was always meant to be there."*
- **Likely project** — Insulated roof, louvered patio, rendered patio, higher-spec carport, verandah.
- **Best proof** — High-quality project photography, finish detail, design consultation process, upgrade options.
- **Risk** — Discount-led messaging can undercut premium confidence if it dominates.

### 3. Practical asset protector

- **Motivation** — Protect valuable assets from sun, storm, rain, hail and everyday exposure.
- **Decision drivers** — Durability, council approvals, structural confidence, matching the home, clear pricing, fast problem-solving.
- **Emotional hook** — *"Protect what matters without making your home look like a shed was slapped onto it."*
- **Likely project** — Carport, double carport, caravan cover, flyover carport, insulated carport.
- **Best proof** — Vehicle-specific project examples, span/height options, engineering reassurance, home-matched finishes.
- **Risk** — Treating carports as only functional misses the customer pride and street-appeal factor.

### 4. Overwhelmed homeowner

- **Motivation** — Get a proper result without personally managing design details, council, engineering, drafting, scheduling and trades.
- **Decision drivers** — Single accountable team, clear steps, communication, design consultation, project management, approvals handled.
- **Emotional hook** — *"We will handle the hard parts."*
- **Likely project** — Any project where the customer has site complexity, approval concerns or low confidence in the building process.
- **Best proof** — Simple process diagrams, clear responsibilities, timelines, communication promises, customer reviews mentioning *"easy"* or *"no fuss"*.
- **Risk** — Too many product options without guidance makes this buyer freeze. Choice is good; *guided* choice is better.
- **Note** — This may be Trueline's biggest commercial opportunity — the segment most willing to pay for the project being managed for them.

### 5. Value-conscious quality buyer

- **Motivation** — Improve the home in a financially sensible way, with confidence the spend is justified.
- **Decision drivers** — Transparent pricing, flexible options, warranties, quality materials, promotion clarity, no nasty surprises.
- **Emotional hook** — *"Designed around your home, lifestyle and budget."*
- **Likely project** — Patios and carports across middle-tier options, possibly influenced by seasonal campaigns.
- **Best proof** — Value explanation, warranty comparison, material benefits, process clarity.
- **Risk** — Leaning too heavily on discounting attracts low-fit price shoppers and undermines the premium position.

## Customer fears → brand response opportunities

When a draft is about reassurance, reach for this matrix. Each fear has a specific lever — name the fear, then show the response.

| Customer fear | Brand response |
|---|---|
| Getting ripped off or oversold | Show clear process and accountability. |
| Poor workmanship | Use reviews that mention ease and confidence. |
| Council or approval problems | Lead with designed-for-your-home outcomes and "we handle approvals". |
| An ugly add-on that hurts the home | Show matched finishes, roofline integration, before/after. |
| Delays and poor communication | Make warranties simple and meaningful; show communication promises. |
| Wrong product choice | Use real project stories by region and need. |
| Spending money and regretting it | Explain choices without overwhelming people; use price/value framing, not cheapness. |
| Nobody taking responsibility if something goes wrong | Show who manages approvals, communication, build and aftercare. |

## What customers are really buying

| Surface product | Emotional purchase | Marketing angle |
|---|---|---|
| Patio | A better everyday lifestyle | The room outside where family, friends and quiet mornings happen. |
| Insulated patio roof | Comfort and year-round use | More usable days, cooler shade, a space that feels part of the home. |
| Carport | Protection and pride | Protect the car, caravan, boat or 4WD without compromising street appeal. |
| Council/approvals handled | Relief | We handle the hard parts so the customer can focus on the outcome. |
| Custom design | Confidence | Designed for your home, not pulled from a catalogue. |

The rule that follows from this table: **lead with the emotional purchase, support with the surface product**. Never the other way round.

## Customer journey — emotion at each stage

Match the emotion at each stage; the biggest mistake is asking for the sale before the customer feels understood and safe.

| Stage | Customer feeling | Marketing job | Best assets |
|---|---|---|---|
| Dreaming | I want more from my home. | Inspire and show what is possible. | Lifestyle reels, lookbooks, before/after galleries, seasonal campaigns. |
| Researching | What type of patio/carport is right? | Educate without overwhelming. | Buyer guides, FAQs, comparison pages, suburb/project examples. |
| Shortlisting | Who can I trust? | Reduce risk and prove credibility. | Reviews, guarantees, process page, team story, licensing, project count. |
| Appointment | Will they understand my home? | Create confidence in advice and process. | Pre-appointment email, designer profile, preparation checklist. |
| Quote decision | Is this worth the investment? | Connect price to outcome, quality and certainty. | Proposal storytelling, product benefits, visual options, financing if applicable. |
| Build phase | I hope this goes smoothly. | Communicate and reassure. | Milestone emails, what-to-expect guides, named contact, progress photos. |
| After completion | I am proud of this. | Turn satisfaction into advocacy. | Review request, referral offer, care guide, photo permission, add-on offers. |

## What this means for any draft

Before you write, name two things:

1. **Which segment** are we speaking to? (If unsure, default to 1.)
2. **Which stage of the journey** are they at?

Then choose the emotional hook from the segment, the response from the fears matrix, and the proof asset from the journey table. That's the spine of the message.
